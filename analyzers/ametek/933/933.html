AMETEK 933 Series (Oxygen Analyzer)

Oxygen measurement system commonly used for combustion control and process monitoring. Typical installations include boilers, furnaces, incinerators, and gas streams where O₂ is critical.

Field tip: If O₂ readings are unstable, check sample system first (leaks, moisture, filters, flow, pressure). Sensor replacement should be the last step after verifying the basics.

Working Principle (General)

The AMETEK 933 Series measures oxygen concentration by sensing oxygen partial pressure or oxygen-related electrochemical response (depending on the specific 933 configuration). The analyzer conditions the sample gas (flow/pressure/temperature) and converts the sensor output into oxygen concentration. Output is typically available as 4–20 mA and/or digital communication for the DCS/PLC.

  • Stable sampling (flow + pressure + dryness) is essential for stable O₂ readings.
  • Calibration validates the measurement scale using known gases (zero/span).
  • Diagnostics usually include sensor health, flow, temperature, and system alarms.
Note: Exact sensor type and calibration details can vary by 933 version. This guide is written to match common field practice. If you share the exact model/options, I can tailor it precisely.

Main Components (Technician View)

  • Analyzer cabinet / transmitter (HMI, controller, I/O)
  • O₂ sensor assembly (measurement cell + wiring)
  • Sample system (filter, regulator, flow control, tubing)
  • Sample pump (if fitted) and moisture separation (if fitted)
  • Heater/temperature control (if sensor requires controlled temperature)
  • Calibration gas manifold (zero/span valves, restrictors)
  • Outputs to DCS (4–20 mA, alarms, Modbus/serial/Ethernet if available)

Calibration Procedure (Zero + Span)

1) Warm-up & stabilize: let the analyzer reach normal operating temperature/ready status.
2) Confirm sample flow: stable flow/pressure and no leaks. Replace/clean filters if DP is high.
3) Apply Zero gas: typically N₂ (or certified low-O₂ gas) as per site practice. Wait for stable reading.
4) Perform Zero adjustment (if your procedure allows). Record pre/post values.
5) Apply Span gas: certified O₂ in N₂ (or air as span if permitted by site SOP). Wait for stabilization.
6) Perform Span adjustment. Record cylinder details (concentration, expiry, certificate).
7) Return to sample and verify: confirm reading returns smoothly and remains stable.
Best practice: If zero/span won’t stabilize, do not force calibration. Fix sample conditioning, leaks, and sensor health first.

Major Troubleshooting (Top Field Issues)

  • Unstable O₂ reading — sample leaks, moisture, unstable flow/pressure, pump issues, loose electrical connections
  • High O₂ bias — air ingress/leaks, cracked tubing, loose fittings, sample dilution
  • Low O₂ bias — blocked filter, low sample flow, wrong calibration gas, span gas depleted
  • Slow response — long sample lines, plugged filters, weak pump, condensation, manifold restriction
  • Calibration failure — wrong gas, leaking cal manifold, sensor aging, insufficient warm-up time
  • Communication/4–20 issues — scaling mismatch, wiring polarity, loop power problems, grounding
Quick check order: 1) flow/pressure 2) leaks 3) moisture/filters 4) calibration gas validity 5) sensor health.
