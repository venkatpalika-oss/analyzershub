Commissioning Failures

Commissioning is where analyzer reliability is either built correctly or damaged permanently. Many analyzers fail repeatedly not because of design, but because of poor first-time setup.

Field reality: A badly commissioned analyzer will never behave like a stable one, no matter how many times it is calibrated.

Typical Symptoms After Startup

  • Analyzer never stabilizes
  • Frequent alarms from day one
  • Calibration values drift rapidly
  • Mismatch between analyzer and lab results
  • Operators lose confidence in readings

Common Commissioning Mistakes

  • Commissioning before sampling system is ready
  • No verification of utilities under load
  • Skipping soak-in and stabilization time
  • Incorrect configuration of ranges and alarms
  • Calibrating immediately after startup
  • Rushing handover to operations

Frequent Commissioning Failure Modes

  • False baseline: analyzer learns wrong “normal”
  • Sensor shock: exposure to harsh gas too early
  • Bad alarm tuning: nuisance alarms from day one
  • Hidden leaks: missed during rushed testing
  • Documentation gaps: future troubleshooting becomes guesswork

Correct Commissioning Sequence

1) Complete mechanical installation & leak checks
2) Verify utilities under actual operating conditions
3) Flush and stabilize sampling system
4) Power up analyzer and allow thermal stabilization
5) Observe trends before any calibration
6) Perform gentle calibration and verification
7) Tune alarms based on real process behavior

Project-Level Causes

  • Commissioning scheduled too early in project timeline
  • Lack of analyzer-specific expertise onsite
  • No ownership between EPC and operations
  • Incomplete documentation and drawings

Best Practices

  • Commission sampling and utilities before analyzer
  • Allow sufficient stabilization time
  • Document “as-left” conditions clearly
  • Train operators during commissioning
  • Do not rush handover
