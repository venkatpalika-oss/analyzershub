CEMS Block Diagram (Simple Explanation)

Stack/Process → Probe → Heated Line → Sample Conditioning (filter/chiller/dryer) → Analyzer (SO₂/NOx/CO/CO₂/O₂) → DAHS/PLC/DCS → Reporting / Historian

Many “analyzer problems” are actually caused by the probe, sample line, conditioning system, utilities, or DAHS signal handling.

Module 1: Key CEMS Terms

Span & Zero

Zero verifies baseline. Span verifies sensitivity. Both must stabilize and be repeatable.

Drift

Change in reading during a defined period. Drift usually points to contamination, leaks, or aging.

Validity / Flags

Data becomes invalid during calibrations, faults, warm-up, or unstable conditions.

Response Time

How fast the analyzer reaches a stable value. Often slowed by sample restrictions or condensation.

Module 2: Wet vs Dry Basis (Field Meaning)

Emission values may be reported on a wet basis (includes water vapor) or a dry basis (water removed). Conditioning method and reporting rules decide which basis is used.

Field tip: If moisture enters the analyzer or sample line, readings can drift, become noisy, or calibration can fail.

Module 3: Sample System Fundamentals

  • Probe plugging → low flow, slow response, unstable readings
  • Cold spots in heated line → condensation → drift/noise
  • Filters → restriction → slow response / pump strain
  • Leaks → air ingress → false oxygen / dilution effects
  • Chiller/dryer issues → water carryover → analyzer damage

Module 4: Calibration Quality (What “Good” Looks Like)

Stable

Reading settles and stays steady during zero/span application.

Repeatable

Repeated span checks give similar results within expected tolerance.

Fast Enough

Response time is consistent; slow response suggests restrictions.

Traceable

Calibration gases are correct, valid, and properly regulated.

If calibration fails repeatedly, suspect sample conditioning and leaks first — not the analyzer electronics.

Module 5: The Technician’s Troubleshooting Method

  1. Confirm the symptom (trend, alarms, process context)
  2. Verify utilities (power, instrument air, temps, flow)
  3. Check sample system (probe → line → conditioning → flow)
  4. Check calibration path (gas, regulator, manifold, valves)
  5. Only then suspect analyzer hardware/software
  6. Verify recovery with stable trend + repeat checks
